What is a lay audit?

Every non-profit association, student nation and housing cooperative needs an auditor to check that the finances are properly managed. The person who performs the review is called a lay auditor – they do not need to be a trained accountant, but still have a legal and moral responsibility to conduct a thorough and independent review.

A complete audit of a small organisation consists of 10 review steps. How deep the analysis can go depends on which documents are available.

About Step 3 – "The result must equal the change in equity"

In all Swedish accounting systems (Fortnox, Visma, Bokio, etc.), liabilities and equity are credit accounts and are shown with a negative sign in exported reports. This means that when equity increases (e.g. after a profit), the figure in the balance sheet becomes a larger negative number.

We always say in plain language that "a positive result increases equity" – and that is true. But in the exported figures, equity appears to decrease (becomes more negative).

There are therefore two formula versions:

Normal presentation (equity shown as a positive number):
Year's result = Δ Equity + Δ Untaxed reserves

Credit convention (equity shown as a negative number – most common in system exports):
Year's result = −(Δ Equity + Δ Untaxed reserves)

Example (Långkatekesen 2025, credit convention): The year's result was −SEK 14,954 (loss). Δ Equity (closing − opening) = +SEK 597 and Δ Investment fund (closing − opening) = +SEK 14,357. Check: −(597 + 14,357) = −14,954 ✅ – everything checks out.

The generated prompt instructs the AI to always identify the sign convention, apply the correct formula, and check whether untaxed reserves exist.
