# Simplified Annual Accounts, Annual Accounts or Annual Report – and When Is a Professional Auditor Actually Required?

*Published on [Controller utan gränser](https://controllerutangranser.wordpress.com)*

---

Over the past year I have worked on auditing the accounts of non-profit associations of quite different characters – a student nation, a First of May association in Lund, and a solar energy association. It is work that demands systematic thinking, financial understanding and, not least, the ability to explain complex rules in an accessible way. And the more I have worked with this, the clearer it has become that there is considerable confusion around a fairly fundamental question: *What kind of document is the association actually required to prepare? And when is a professional auditor required?*

In this post I will try to untangle the concepts. (See also a [general description of the audit process](https://kentlundgren.github.io/foreningar/KalmarNation/revision/generellt_eng.html) in English.)

---

## Three Levels – and the Logic Behind Them

Swedish regulations divide organisations' accounting obligations into three levels: the simplified annual accounts, the annual accounts, and the annual report. Which level applies is determined primarily by the size of the organisation, measured as net turnover, total assets, and number of employees – and to some extent by the legal form of the organisation.

What confuses many people is that these three concepts are often used interchangeably in everyday speech. It is not uncommon to see a non-profit association call its document an "annual report" when, in strict legal terms, it is simply a set of *accounts*. This is a common mistake – and one I have encountered in practice.

---

## Level 1: The Simplified Annual Accounts

The simplest form of accounting is the **simplified annual accounts**, regulated by the Swedish Accounting Standards Board's general guidance **BFNAR 2010:1** (*Non-profit associations and registered religious communities that prepare simplified annual accounts*). This framework applies to non-profit associations and registered religious communities whose **net turnover normally does not exceed three million Swedish kronor**.

What is required? In essence, only an income statement and a balance sheet – in simplified form. Notes can be kept brief. The idea is to keep the administrative burden manageable for the small association with limited resources.

This is a reasonable level for, for example, a small village association, a minor trade society, or a sports club with limited activity. But as soon as turnover passes three million kronor – a threshold that many active associations do in fact reach – different rules apply.

---

## Level 2: The Annual Accounts

The next level is the **annual accounts**, regulated by **BFNAR 2017:3** (*Annual Accounts*). This applies to organisations whose net turnover exceeds three million kronor but which do not reach the threshold for being a "larger association" (more on that shortly). Unlike the simplified annual accounts, a more complete income statement and balance sheet are required here, supplemented by **additional disclosures** – that is, notes explaining accounting principles, depreciation, material items and the like.

This is the level at which **Kalmar Nation in Lund** finds itself. The nation's net turnover amounts to just over 4 million kronor (2025), which consistently places it above the three-million threshold. This means Kalmar Nation is required to prepare a K2-based **annual accounts** document under BFNAR 2017:3 – not simplified annual accounts, and not a full annual report.

An interesting comparative example appears here: a cold-water bathing association that I have come into contact with through audit work. The association has a net turnover hovering around exactly the three-million threshold – 3.4 million kronor in 2024 and 3.0 million kronor in 2025. Strictly speaking, the association ought to apply BFNAR 2017:3 and prepare a K2-based **annual accounts** document, since its turnover exceeds three million kronor in both years. The association states in its supplementary disclosures that it follows BFNAR 2010:1 (the simplified framework) but has simultaneously "chosen to prepare **annual accounts**, audited by an authorised auditor" – a formulation that in itself reveals an awareness that a borderline situation exists. Furthermore, the document is called an "annual report" in the filename and in the signature section, even though it is neither formally nor substantively an annual report. This is an instructive practical example of how the concepts blur together.

---

## Level 3: The Annual Report

The third and most demanding level is the **annual report**, regulated by the **Annual Accounts Act (ÅRL, 1995:1554)**. An annual report is required of organisations that meet at least **two of three** criteria to be considered a "larger association" (ÅRL 1:3):

- More than **50 employees** (on average during each of the two most recent financial years)
- Total assets exceeding **40 million kronor**
- Net turnover exceeding **80 million kronor**

An annual report contains – in addition to income statement and balance sheet – a **directors' report**, notes, and in some cases a cash flow statement. This is a considerably heavier administrative requirement, and for organisations that reach this threshold there is a further choice to make: should one apply **K2** (BFNAR 2016:10, *Annual Report in Smaller Companies*) or **K3** (BFNAR 2012:1, *Annual Report and Consolidated Financial Statements*)? K2 is simpler and more rule-based; K3 is more principles-based and allows greater flexibility.

Neither Kalmar Nation nor the cold-water bathing association comes anywhere near these thresholds. The annual report is therefore not relevant to them – despite the fact that this is precisely the word that sometimes appears in their documents.

---

## A Note on Regulatory Changes from 2026

The regulatory framework is not static. In June 2025, the Swedish Accounting Standards Board decided on amendments to K2 (BFNAR 2016:10) and K3 (BFNAR 2012:1) that take effect for financial years beginning after 31 December 2025 – that is, from financial year 2026 for most organisations (Bokföringsnämnden, 2025).

The most significant change is that **tenant-owner associations (bostadsrättsföreningar) and housing associations** may no longer apply K2. From 2026, they must prepare their annual report under K3. **Property companies** whose buildings generate at least 75 per cent of net turnover are also required to switch to K3, provided they are not among the very smallest (those that do not exceed more than one of the following conditions: 3 employees, 1.5 million kronor in total assets, or 3 million kronor in net turnover).

For the non-profit associations discussed in this article – which prepare **annual accounts** under BFNAR 2017:3 rather than an annual report – these changes do not have a direct impact. K2 and K3 govern annual reports; BFNAR 2017:3 for annual accounts is a separate framework and has not been amended in this context. But the changes illustrate well that it pays to keep an eye on the Swedish Accounting Standards Board's publications – the regulatory framework is updated, and it happens faster than many people expect.

## When Is a Professional Auditor Actually Required?

This is the question that surprises many people when I raise it: for non-profit associations there is **no statutory obligation to engage an authorised or approved auditor** – regardless of turnover. The Auditing Act (Revisionslagen, 1999:1079) does not apply to non-profit associations as such.

The requirement for professional audit in Sweden follows primarily the **legal form** of the organisation, not its size. For **limited companies (aktiebolag)**, the audit obligation is regulated by the Companies Act (Aktiebolagslagen, 2005:551) Chapter 9 and the Auditing Act (Revisionslagen, 1999:1079). Since 2010, the very smallest private limited companies have been exempt, but companies exceeding the thresholds (broadly: more than 3 employees, total assets above 1.5 million kronor, net turnover above 3 million kronor – in each of the two most recent financial years) are subject to mandatory audit and must engage an authorised or approved auditor. For **economic associations (ekonomiska föreningar)**, the Act on Economic Associations (Lag 2018:672 om ekonomiska föreningar) applies, with similar logic.

But a non-profit association – whether it runs a cold-water bathing facility, a café, or a student nation with a turnover of four million kronor – is not legally required to have a professional audit.

There are, however, situations in which the requirement for a professional auditor arises **in practice**:

**The association's own statutes** may prescribe an authorised auditor. This is a choice the association made at a general meeting, and it is fully binding internally.

**Grant-giving bodies** may require certified audit as a condition for payment – Sida, Vinnova, EU funds, and municipal grant bodies often impose such conditions.

**Banks and lenders** may require it as part of a loan agreement.

**Voluntary choice** – as in the case of the cold-water bathing association, which states explicitly in its supplementary disclosures that it has "chosen to prepare annual accounts, audited by an authorised auditor, even though the association is *only required* to prepare simplified annual accounts." This is a prudent choice given that the association owns property, has a bank loan, and has total assets of just over nine million kronor.

## The Lay Auditor – What Is It, and What Is Required?

In a non-profit association, the person elected by the general meeting to audit the accounts is formally a **revisor** (auditor) – this is the word used in the statutes. In everyday speech, this person is often referred to as a **lekmannarevisor** (lay auditor), to distinguish them from an authorised or approved auditor with formal qualifications and professional titles.

It is worth noting that the term *lekmannarevisor* carries a slightly different technical meaning in the **Companies Act** (ABL Chapter 10), where it refers to a specific role for companies not subject to mandatory professional audit. For non-profit associations, the term is therefore more of a widely used colloquial concept – but a well-established one.

**What is required of a lay auditor in a non-profit association?** There is no law that specifies the requirements in detail, but a number of fundamental principles apply:

There is no requirement for formal education in accounting or auditing. The lay auditor need not be an economist, controller, or professional auditor by trade.

The lay auditor must be **independent** – they should not sit on the board, should not have close family relationships with those who manage the finances, and should not have personal financial interests in the organisation that could affect their judgement.

Despite the absence of formal educational requirements, the lay auditor has a **legal and moral responsibility** to carry out the review diligently. Good auditing practice (*god revisionssed*) serves as a benchmark, even if it is not legally mandated for non-profit associations (FAR, 2024).

The lay auditor must have sufficient **understanding of the accounts** to form a well-founded opinion. It is not enough to sign the audit report without having reviewed the material. An auditor who signs without having conducted a genuine review risks having failed in their duty of loyalty to the association's members.

**The review has two parts**: the lay auditor examines both the accounts (the bookkeeping, supporting documents, bank reconciliation, and the accuracy of the balance sheet) – and the governance (that the board has acted in accordance with the statutes and the decisions of the general meeting).

## What Do the Examples Tell Us?

What I find fascinating about the two examples is that they illustrate the grey area that exists within the regulatory framework.

**Kalmar Nation** has a turnover of 4.3 million kronor and is required to apply BFNAR 2017:3 (K2-based annual accounts). The nation is reviewed by a lay auditor. No authorised auditor is required.

**The cold-water bathing association** literally sits at the boundary: with a turnover of 3.0–3.4 million kronor, the association finds itself in the grey zone between simplified annual accounts (BFNAR 2010:1) and K2-based annual accounts (BFNAR 2017:3). And despite the fact that neither a full annual accounts document nor an annual report is strictly required, the association has voluntarily chosen to engage an authorised auditor. The document is furthermore incorrectly labelled an "annual report" – which is technically not correct, and an example of how the conceptual confusion persists in practice.

The concepts of simplified annual accounts, annual accounts, and annual report are therefore not merely academic distinctions. They have real consequences for what must be included in the document, which regulatory framework governs it, and – indirectly – what type of reviewer is needed.

---

## Summary

| Level | Framework | Applies when | Professional auditor required? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Simplified annual accounts | BFNAR 2010:1 | Non-profit association, net turnover ≤ SEK 3m | No |
| Annual accounts | BFNAR 2017:3 | Net turnover > SEK 3m, not a "larger association" | No (for non-profit associations) |
| Annual report | ÅRL (1995:1554), K2/K3 | "Larger association": 2 of 3 criteria met | No (for non-profit associations) – but common in practice |

And the most important takeaway: **in Sweden, the requirement for a professional auditor follows the legal form of the organisation, not its size**. A non-profit association can have a turnover of tens of millions of kronor without being legally required to engage an authorised auditor. But this does not mean that avoiding one is a wise choice – it is something to weigh against the organisation's complexity, risk profile, and the requirements imposed by grant-giving bodies and lenders.
